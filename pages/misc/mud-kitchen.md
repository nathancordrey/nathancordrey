# Cedar Mud Kitchen Build Guide

![Cedar mud kitchen concept render](static/images/misc/mud-kitchen-render.png)

A simple, durable outdoor mud kitchen using a **cedar frame** and an **acetylated wood benchtop**.

## Project overview

This design aims for the sweet spot between durability, cost, and simplicity:

- **Frame:** cedar
- **Benchtop:** acetylated wood
- **Overall size:** about **48" W x 20" D x 36" H**
- **Counter height:** about **24"**
- **Style:** open shelf, removable sink bin, no fake oven doors or other fragile extras

## Recommended final dimensions

- **Overall width:** 48"
- **Overall depth:** 20"
- **Overall height:** 36"
- **Counter height:** 24"
- **Back rail / splash height above counter:** 10" to 12"
- **Sink opening:** sized to your bin or bowl
- **Open work area:** roughly 28" to 30" of clear space beside the sink

## Parts list

### Lumber

#### Cedar
- 4 legs at about **2x2 or 4x4**, cut to final height needs
- 2 front/back upper aprons
- 2 side upper aprons
- 2 front/back lower shelf rails
- 2 side lower shelf rails
- 1 back rail / splash board
- Optional 1 small upper shelf
- Cedar slats for the lower shelf

#### Acetylated wood
- 1 benchtop panel or enough boards to make a top about **48" x 20"**

### Hardware
- Exterior-grade **stainless steel screws**
- Exterior wood glue, optional
- 4 to 6 stainless hooks, optional
- 2 small angle brackets for an upper shelf, optional

### Sink / play pieces
- 1 removable sink bin, bus tub, or stainless bowl
- Optional small pots, spoons, and measuring cups

### Base / site prep
- Pavers, gravel, mulch, or another well-draining base

## Suggested cut list

This is a practical first-pass cut list. Adjust based on actual stock sizes and your sink dimensions.

### Cedar frame
- 4 legs @ **36"**
- 2 upper aprons @ **45"**
- 2 upper side aprons @ **17"**
- 2 lower shelf rails @ **45"**
- 2 lower shelf side rails @ **17"**
- 1 back splash rail @ **48"**
- 5 to 7 lower shelf slats @ about **45"**
- Optional upper mini-shelf @ about **20" to 24"**

### Acetylated top
- 1 panel or glued-up top @ **48" x 20"**

> Note: apron lengths assume the top overhangs the frame slightly. Adjust to your actual lumber thickness.

## Tools

- Tape measure
- Pencil
- Speed square
- Saw
- Drill/driver
- Drill bits and countersink bit
- Sander or sanding block
- Jigsaw for the sink cutout
- Clamps, helpful but not mandatory

## Build steps

### 1. Build the upper frame
Build a simple rectangular frame for the countertop using the cedar upper aprons and side aprons.

- Keep it square
- Predrill all screw holes
- Use stainless screws

### 2. Attach the legs
Fasten the 4 cedar legs to the upper frame.

- Final counter height should land around **24"**
- If your legs run full height to support the backsplash, that is fine too

### 3. Add the lower shelf frame
Install the lower shelf rails several inches above the ground.

- Leave enough clearance below for drainage and cleanup
- A lower shelf height around **6" to 8" off the ground** works well

### 4. Install the lower shelf slats
Fasten cedar shelf slats across the lower frame.

- Leave small gaps between slats for drainage
- Keep it simple and sturdy

### 5. Prepare and install the benchtop
Cut the acetylated top to size and dry-fit it.

- Mark your sink opening
- Cut the sink opening with a jigsaw
- Sand the edges lightly
- Fasten the top to the cedar frame with stainless screws from underneath when possible for a cleaner look

### 6. Add the backsplash / back rail
Install a cedar back rail or simple splash at the rear.

- This adds visual structure
- It also gives you a place for hooks or a small shelf

### 7. Add optional shelf and hooks
If desired, add a narrow cedar shelf and a few hooks.

- Keep this minimal
- Too many accessories make it busier and less durable

### 8. Fit the sink bin
Drop in the removable sink bin or bowl.

- A bus tub is practical and easy to replace
- A stainless bowl also works well

### 9. Final sanding and setup
Break sharp edges with sandpaper and set the kitchen on a draining base.

- Best base: pavers, gravel, mulch, or similar
- Avoid setting the legs directly in wet soil

## Build notes

- **Cedar for the structure** keeps the cost reasonable and is already a strong outdoor choice.
- **Acetylated wood for the top** gives you premium durability where kids will dump water and mud the most.
- **Open shelving** is better than enclosed cabinets outdoors.
- **Simple is durable.** Skip fake burners, oven doors, canopies, and decorative trim.

## Finish recommendations

You can leave both woods more natural, but a few practical notes:

- Cedar can be left unfinished and will weather to a gray tone over time.
- If you want to preserve color, use an exterior finish appropriate for cedar.
- Follow the acetylated wood manufacturer's finish guidance if you choose to coat the top.
- Avoid film finishes that may peel if you expect heavy water and mud use.

## Safety notes

- Sand corners and edges so they are not sharp.
- Use stainless hardware to reduce rust staining.
- Make sure the unit is stable and does not rack side to side.
- Check screws occasionally as the piece settles outdoors.

## Nice optional upgrades

- A second removable bin on the lower shelf
- A few simple tool hooks
- A small top shelf for cups
- Locking casters only if you truly need mobility

## What to avoid

- Thin decorative doors
- Complex toy stovetops
- Fabric canopies
- Tight waterproof tarps that trap moisture
- Bare steel fasteners

## Shopping summary

If you want the simplest buying plan:

- Cedar boards for the frame and shelf
- One acetylated board or top panel for the benchtop
- Stainless screws
- One removable bin or stainless bowl
- Optional hooks
- Gravel or pavers for the base

## Final thought

This hybrid design should feel bigger and sturdier than many store-bought mud kitchens while staying much more practical than building the whole thing from acetylated wood.
